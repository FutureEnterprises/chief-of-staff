# Behavioral support did not slow GLP-1 regain. Here's what will.

*Iman Schrock, COYL · May 2026 · Substack + LinkedIn*

---

The most important sentence in obesity medicine this year is buried in
an Oxford/BMJ meta-analysis from January.

> "The failure of existing behavioral programs to slow regain rate after
> GLP-1 cessation was one of the more disappointing findings."

Read that twice.

For thirty years the dominant model of weight management was: educate
the patient, hold them accountable weekly, give them a coach to call.
Noom built a $3.7B company on that model. WeightWatchers built a
sixty-year brand on it. Calibrate raised $127M on it.

Then GLP-1s arrived. Patients lost 17% of their body weight. The
behavioral programs were repositioned as the maintenance layer — they
would keep the weight off. Drug does the work; coaching keeps the
result. That was the deal.

The data is in. The coaching does not keep the result.

[SURMOUNT-4's post-hoc analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41284285/),
published in February, showed **82% of tirzepatide stoppers regained
more than 25% of their lost weight within a year of stopping the drug.**
[The JAMA Network Open cohort](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2829779)
of 125,474 patients shows **53.6% discontinue within 12 months. 72.2%
within 24.** The patients in those cohorts who had weekly coaching,
monthly check-ins, food logging, peer community, accountability text
messages — they regained at almost the same rate as the patients who
had nothing.

Oxford's words again: *one of the more disappointing findings.*

So let's be disappointed. And let's be honest about why.

## The 9 PM fridge raid is not a knowledge problem

The patient knows they shouldn't eat the leftovers. They've read the
leptin chapter. Their coach told them this morning that the danger
window is the four hours after dinner.

None of that information is available to them at 9:04 PM when their
hand is on the fridge handle and their brain has produced the sentence
*I deserve this* for the third time this week.

What catches the 9 PM raid is not curriculum. It is a precisely-timed
interrupt at 9:03:47, before the hand moves, that says: *you're not
hungry. This is the pattern. You said you wanted to be the person who
closed the fridge tonight. Here's the 30-second exercise. Then decide.*

Weekly coaching can't do that. Daily check-ins can't do that. A
biweekly clinician visit can't do that. The architecture of
asynchronous coaching is fundamentally mismatched to the architecture
of behavioral relapse — which is real-time, embodied, and over in
three seconds.

The science for what fits the moment exists. Behavioral medicine calls
it **just-in-time adaptive intervention**, or JITAI. Inbal Nahum-Shani
and her colleagues defined it in 2018 in
[Annals of Behavioral Medicine](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27663578/).
A JITAI fires precisely when the user is in the state-of-need AND the
state-of-receptivity at the same time. Not when it's convenient for
the coach. Not when it's scheduled.

The science has been mature for seven years. The consumer-grade
implementation, in the GLP-1 maintenance window, has not existed.

---

## What real-time actually means

Most apps that say "real-time" mean your phone vibrates when they feel
like it. That isn't what real-time means in JITAI.

Real-time means the interrupt fires within three seconds of the cue,
before the action runs to completion. Three seconds is not arbitrary —
it's the window the behavioral-science literature identifies as the
moment a cued behavior can still be re-routed. After three seconds you
are not interrupting a behavior; you are interrupting the explanation
of one. Different and less useful job.

To fire in three seconds, the system has to:

- **Detect the danger window** — time of day, context, prior-pattern
  match. A patient whose binge always happens between 8:45 and 9:30 PM
  after a high-stress workday is not in the same state at 9:04 PM as
  at 11 AM the next morning.
- **Detect the script loading** — the lock-screen interaction pattern,
  the tab switch toward DoorDash, the explicit cue ("I need help"),
  the absence of expected cues ("they always brush their teeth by 9:30
  and it's 9:42").
- **Choose the right intervention for this patient** — what works on
  the 9 PM Negotiator is different from what works on the Stress
  Rebounder is different from what works on the Reward Rebounder. One
  size of script fits exactly nobody.
- **Deliver it in a way they'll accept** — not a patronizing nag, not
  a scold, not a notification that looks like every other notification
  on the phone. Specific, named, in-context.
- **Learn from what happened next** — did they close the fridge? Did
  they open it anyway? Did they text a friend? Did they go to bed? The
  system has to get sharper at this specific patient every cycle.

That's the architecture. It's not coaching. It's not curriculum. It's
not a chatbot. It's a sensor-driven, archetype-aware, sub-three-second
interrupt that lives on the patient's phone and gets smarter every
cycle.
