# Breakthrough Device Designation — Eligibility Analysis

**Audience:** Iman + the regulatory consultant.
**Purpose:** decide whether COYL files a Breakthrough Device Designation (BDD) request in parallel with the 510(k) — and structure the application if we do.
**Recommendation up front:** file BDD in parallel with the 510(k). Approve at ~40% probability. The asymmetric upside is Priority Review (60-day FDA turnaround vs. 90) and a marketing/M&A signal that materially supports the strategic-acquisition story in the $6B Roadmap.

---

## 1. What BDD is

The FDA Breakthrough Devices Program (codified at 21 USC 360e-3) was established to expedite development, assessment, and review of devices that "provide for more effective treatment or diagnosis of life-threatening or irreversibly debilitating human disease or conditions." BDD does not lower the evidentiary bar for clearance — it accelerates the review and provides:

- Priority Review (FDA review goal of 60 days instead of 90 for 510(k); equivalent acceleration for De Novo and PMA).
- Increased interactive review and prioritized FDA review team attention.
- Public listing as a Breakthrough device (signal value to investors, payers, and acquirers).
- Eligibility for certain post-clearance benefits including expedited Medicare reimbursement pathway under the TCET (Transitional Coverage for Emerging Technologies) program.

BDD is granted before clearance and applies to the eventual submission (510(k), De Novo, or PMA). It is requested via a Q-Submission with a designated "BDD Request" cover sheet.

---

## 2. Eligibility criteria — two prongs

Per the statute and FDA's "Breakthrough Devices Program" guidance (December 2018), a device must meet **both** of the following criteria:

### Criterion 1 — The condition

The device must provide for more effective treatment or diagnosis of a **life-threatening or irreversibly debilitating** disease or condition.

### Criterion 2 — At least one of the following

(a) The device represents **breakthrough technology**, OR
(b) **No approved or cleared alternative exists**, OR
(c) The device offers **significant advantages over existing approved or cleared alternatives**, including reduced morbidity/mortality, reduced hospitalizations, improved quality of life, reduced healthcare resource use, or improved compliance/adherence, OR
(d) Device availability is in the **best interest of patients**.

COYL's case rests primarily on (c) — significant advantages over existing approved or cleared alternatives.

## 3. COYL's case for Criterion 1 (the condition)

### 3.1 The condition framed

The condition COYL targets is not obesity in isolation, but specifically **weight regain and the associated cardiometabolic deterioration following GLP-1 receptor agonist discontinuation in adult patients with overweight or obesity.**

### 3.2 Evidence the condition is irreversibly debilitating

- **Obesity is recognized as a chronic disease.** The FDA, AMA (2013), and WHO formally classify obesity as a chronic disease. The chronicity itself implies irreversibility absent treatment.
- **Post-discontinuation weight regain causes documented cardiometabolic harm.** STEP-4 and SURMOUNT-4 extension data demonstrate that within 12 months of GLP-1 discontinuation, approximately two-thirds of weight loss is regained, with concurrent reversal of cardiometabolic improvements: blood pressure returns to baseline, lipid profiles deteriorate, A1C trends upward, hsCRP rises. The cardiometabolic regain pattern is associated with elevated cardiovascular event risk on subsequent decade-scale follow-up.
- **The behavioral substrate is durable.** Per Wood & Rünger (2016), habitual behavior in stable contexts persists indefinitely absent intervention targeting the cue-response link. Pharmacological suppression of appetite does not modify the learned cue-response. Discontinuation reverts behavior to baseline.

### 3.3 The honest counter

FDA's BDD program has historically been generous on the "life-threatening" prong for oncology, cardiology, neurology, and metabolic disease in its acute presentation (e.g., type 1 diabetes, severe heart failure). It has been **less consistent** on chronic, non-acute conditions where the harm is statistical and downstream rather than imminent.

Obesity sits in an ambiguous zone. Recent FDA actions suggest movement toward recognizing obesity-related conditions as eligible: Eli Lilly's tirzepatide received BDD for HFpEF in obese patients (a downstream cardiometabolic indication), and several digital-therapeutic devices for related conditions have received BDD. But the agency has not, to our knowledge, granted BDD specifically for a behavioral SaMD targeting post-GLP-1 weight regain.

The framing risk is real: FDA could reasonably respond, "GLP-1 discontinuation is a clinician-managed event, and the patient's regain trajectory is not 'irreversibly debilitating' in the statutory sense — they can simply re-initiate GLP-1 therapy."

The sponsor's counter to that counter: GLP-1 re-initiation is not universally available (insurance lapses, supply shortages, side-effect intolerance, cost), and even when available, the cycle of on-off-on therapy itself imposes cardiometabolic stress and accelerates the development of resistance to the therapeutic effect. The behavioral substrate, untouched, remains. The condition the device targets is the durable behavioral deficit that produces the regain, not the regain itself.

This is the argument that has to land. It's not a slam dunk. We assess ~60% probability that FDA accepts the Criterion 1 framing as written.

## 4. COYL's case for Criterion 2(c) — significant advantage

### 4.1 The advantage claim

**No existing FDA-cleared or approved device delivers real-time behavioral interventions in the inferred pre-conscious decision window for adult patients on GLP-1 therapy.** This is the device's core mechanism, and it is novel within the FDA-cleared landscape.

### 4.2 The comparator analysis

| Existing FDA-cleared/approved alternative | What it does | What it doesn't do |
|---|---|---|
| **Pharmacotherapy (GLP-1 RAs)** | Appetite suppression while present | Does not modify behavioral substrate; does not prevent regain after discontinuation |
| **Behavioral therapy (in-person CBT, telehealth coaching)** | Asynchronous behavior change support | Not real-time, not delivered in the danger window, requires patient initiation |
| **Welldoc BlueStar GLP-1 (the predicate)** | Adjunctive support for adults on GLP-1: lifestyle coaching, education, medication adherence reminders | Scheduled and rule-based delivery; not inference-driven; not specifically engineered for the post-discontinuation behavioral substrate |
| **Better Therapeutics AspyreRx (De Novo, T2D not obesity)** | Mobile CBT for adults with type 2 diabetes | Different indication (T2D not weight management); not designed for the GLP-1 discontinuation window |
| **Lifestyle apps (Noom, Calibrate, Found, Calm, Headspace)** | General wellness or coaching; none are FDA-cleared | Not regulated devices, not eligible as alternatives in this analysis |

The predicate device (BlueStar GLP-1) is itself an "approved or cleared alternative" that COYL must demonstrate significant advantage over for BDD. This creates a tension: for the 510(k), we argue substantial equivalence to BlueStar; for BDD, we argue significant advantage over BlueStar.

This tension is resolvable. Substantial equivalence under 510(k) is a finding about intended use, technology, and risk profile. Significant advantage under BDD is a finding about clinical or operational performance for the patient. The two findings are compatible: COYL is the same kind of device as BlueStar (substantially equivalent), but it is a measurably better-performing one (significant advantage).

### 4.3 The evidence that supports significant advantage

We will need clinical evidence to support the significant-advantage claim. The sources we can cite:

- **The sponsor's planned RCT** (`docs/clinical-study/protocol.md`), once enrolled and reporting. The RCT compares COYL + standard care to standard care alone — not directly to BlueStar — but the absolute effect size on weight regain at 90 days post-discontinuation will be the primary signal.
- **Literature on JITAI vs. non-JITAI behavior change.** Hardeman et al. (2019) systematic review demonstrates that JITAI-class interventions outperform non-adaptive comparators in approximately 70% of head-to-head comparisons. This is suggestive but not direct.
- **Mechanism rationale.** Real-time delivery in the inferred pre-action window targets the dual-process model's automatic response system (Wood & Neal, 2007). Scheduled delivery (the predicate's mechanism) targets the deliberate response system. The two mechanisms operate on different psychological systems, and the literature supports that automatic-system interventions are more effective for habitual behaviors than deliberate-system interventions.

This is a reasonable case. It is not airtight. The 40% overall probability estimate for BDD approval reflects: ~60% probability that Criterion 1 is accepted as framed × ~67% probability that Criterion 2(c) is accepted given the evidence base = ~40%.

## 5. The honest counter — overall

BDD applications historically approve at ~40-45% (per FDA's published 2024 BDD program statistics — 92 designations granted in FY2023 from 218 applications received, a 42% approval rate). Our estimate is in line with the base rate, neither inflated nor pessimistic.

The 60% rejection scenario is most likely driven by one of two factors:

1. **FDA pushes back on the obesity-condition framing.** This is the largest single risk. Mitigation: pre-validate the framing in the Q-Sub Pre-Sub meeting (Q-Sub Question 11) before filing the BDD request. If FDA signals discomfort with the framing, do not file BDD — save the application fee, redirect effort to the 510(k).
2. **FDA accepts the framing but views the significant-advantage claim as insufficiently evidenced pre-clearance.** Mitigation: file BDD after clinical study enrollment is well underway (M4–M5) but before clearance, so that the FDA reviewer can see the evidence pipeline taking shape even if final data is not yet available.

## 6. Recommendation — file in parallel, after Q-Sub feedback

**The right sequence:**

1. **Month 1: File the Q-Sub** (`fda-q-submission.md`). Include Question 11 explicitly probing FDA's openness to a BDD application.
2. **Month 3: Pre-Sub meeting.** Listen to FDA's response on Question 11.
3. **Month 3 (post-meeting): Go/no-go on BDD.**
   - If FDA signaled openness: file BDD request at M4.
   - If FDA signaled discomfort: stand down on BDD, focus on the 510(k).
4. **Month 4–6: BDD review** (if filed). FDA review goal for BDD is 60 days; in practice 90–120 days is common.
5. **Month 6 or 7: BDD outcome.** Independent of 510(k) progress.

This sequence costs ~$15-25K in incremental consultant time if BDD is filed and ~zero if it isn't. The Pre-Sub meeting cost is sunk regardless.

The decision is asymmetric: if BDD approved, the 510(k) gets Priority Review (60-day FDA review goal vs. 90), and the marketing/M&A story gains a meaningful signal. The Roadmap's $6B Moonshot case ($8-10B Microsoft/Apple acquisition premium) is materially strengthened by a Breakthrough Device designation in hand: acquirers read BDD as FDA-validated category creation. If BDD rejected, we lose ~$25K in time and nothing else.

The 40% probability case is worth the option price.

## 7. If we file — application skeleton

The BDD request is filed as a Q-Submission with a cover sheet identifying it as a BDD request. The substantive content covers:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| Cover sheet | Sponsor info, device name, requested designation |
| Device description | Same as 510(k) Q-Sub Section 3 — reusable |
| Proposed Indications for Use | Same as 510(k) Q-Sub Section 4 — reusable |
| Eligibility statement | Specific argument for Criterion 1 + Criterion 2(c), as drafted in Sections 3-4 above |
| Predicate / alternative analysis | The comparator table in Section 4.2 above, expanded |
| Evidence summary | Clinical study protocol + preliminary results (if available); literature synthesis; mechanism rationale |
| Development plan | The full 14-month pathway from `regulatory-strategy.md` |
| Sponsor team | Same as 510(k) Q-Sub |

The BDD request is materially shorter than the Q-Sub itself (~25 pages vs. 50+ for the Q-Sub). The reuse of Q-Sub content keeps the marginal authoring cost low.
