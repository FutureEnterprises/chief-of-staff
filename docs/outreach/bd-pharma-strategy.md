## Section 1 — Strategic frame: the parallel-bidder dynamic

The structural reality of strategic-bidder M&A: a single bidder
prices on standalone value. Two bidders price on the cost of losing.

| Scenario | Bidders | Pricing logic | Likely outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single bidder (Novo only) | 1 | Standalone strategic value to Novo | $1-2B |
| Two-bidder parallel | 2 | Cost-of-losing to second-place bidder | $4-6B |
| Three-bidder + tech-platform overlap | 3 | Strategic + technology premium stack | $5-8B |

The $4-6B premium is not a function of COYL's intrinsic value (which
is what the standalone single-bidder case prices). It's a function
of what Lilly loses if Novo gets COYL (the maintenance + multi-
indication retention edge for the next decade) and vice versa.

This is the same dynamic that drove Manus → Meta at $2B (16× ARR)
because Microsoft was already piloting Manus inside Windows 11.
Microsoft was the "second-place loser" in that deal. The price
reflects what Microsoft lost by not winning, not what Manus was
worth on its own.

**The strategic premium math for COYL:**
- Standalone strategic value to Novo: ~$1-2B (the maintenance-
  adjunct commercial case)
- Standalone strategic value to Lilly: ~$1-2B (the retention-
  extension commercial case)
- Premium driven by parallel-bidder dynamic: $2-4B incremental
- Total acquisition price band when parallel works: $4-6B

The premium math holds only if both Novo and Lilly are in genuine
parallel conversations. One conversation = one price. Two
conversations = two prices stacking on each other. The mechanic of
this BD strategy is engineering the parallel.

### Why this specifically applies to COYL right now

Three conditions need to be true for the parallel-bidder mechanic
to work:

1. **The acquirers have a structural reason to compete.** Novo + Lilly
   are in a literal commercial war over GLP-1 share. Both are
   spending billions on commercial launch + manufacturing capacity.
   Both are looking for the next-decade moat beyond molecule
   selection.

2. **The asset is meaningfully strategic to both.** COYL fits
   structurally for both: maintenance-adjunct (Novo's gap),
   retention-extension (Lilly's edge). The fit is symmetric, which
   means neither acquirer can rationalize "let the other side
   have it."

3. **The timing is now, not later.** The window for category
   capture in behavioral GLP-1 adjuncts is 12-18 months. After
   that, one of the following is true: (a) Novo or Lilly built
   their own, (b) Noom Med or another player signed exclusive with
   one side, (c) the category commoditizes through generic
   compounders. The premium evaporates after the window closes.

All three conditions are true today. They will not be true in 2028.

## Section 2 — Timing: the GLP-1 commodity wave

The GLP-1 obesity wave is mid-cycle in May 2026. The drugs themselves
are out of the early-launch premium phase and entering the
commodity + competition phase. Three timing markers matter:

**Marker 1 — Patent cliff trajectory.** Semaglutide goes off-patent
in 2031. Tirzepatide in 2036. The runway to commercial moat through
non-molecule differentiation (formulation, delivery, behavioral
adjunct, evidence-of-outcomes) is the next 3-5 years. Both Novo and
Lilly have explicitly named "behavioral adjunct" as a defensive
strategy in their public investor materials.

**Marker 2 — Compounding pharmacy + generic pressure.** Compounding
versions of semaglutide are taking ~5-10% of the addressable share
already, and the generic launch in 2031-2032 will accelerate the
commoditization. The molecule's commercial moat erodes. The
behavioral + service layer becomes the differentiation.

**Marker 3 — Payer coverage tightening.** Payers are moving from
"any GLP-1 covered" to "outcomes-based GLP-1 coverage." That means:
"Show us 12-month and 24-month sustained weight loss data, or we
tier the drug or stop covering it." Sustained outcomes require
behavioral support. Pharma needs the behavioral evidence layer for
payer coverage maintenance.

All three markers converge into a 12-18 month window where pharma is
maximally motivated to acquire the behavioral interrupt platform.
That window is May 2026 → late 2027.

**The window closes when:**
- (a) Either Novo or Lilly has built or acquired a behavioral adjunct
  (most likely by Q4 2027)
- (b) The molecule-side commercial pressure stabilizes (generic
  launches push behavioral adjunct from "high priority" to "table
  stakes")
- (c) A competitor (Noom Med, Calibrate Health, Sequence) signs an
  exclusive partnership with one of the two acquirers (this is the
  highest-risk path; see Section 4)

## Section 3 — Sequencing: Novo first, Lilly 30 days later

The sequencing of the two pharma conversations is the most important
operational decision in this strategy. Wrong sequence kills the
parallel-bidder dynamic. Right sequence creates it.

### Why Novo first

Three reasons Novo is the first conversation:

1. **Higher financial capacity.** Novo's market cap is ~$500B+;
   Lilly's is ~$700B but Novo has the more concentrated obesity
   commercial moat, which means a larger share of Novo's
   incremental capital is allocable to obesity. Novo can absorb a
   $4-6B acquisition more readily than Lilly's BU-fragmented
   capital allocation.

2. **Incumbent positioning.** Wegovy launched first. Novo is the
   incumbent obesity GLP-1 leader. Incumbents are more sensitive
   to category-defining acquisitions by competitors than challengers
   are. Novo is the more motivated bidder when the conversation
   includes "Lilly might buy this."

3. **Slower default tempo.** Novo's corp dev moves at a slower pace
   (90-day LOI cadence vs Lilly's 60-75 days). Starting with Novo
   gives us the time to build the parallel pressure before Lilly's
   faster process collapses the timeline.

### Why Lilly 30 days later

Three reasons the gap is 30 days, not 7 and not 90:

1. **Long enough for Novo to have a real reaction.** Novo's first
   30 days post-cold-email are: review packet, route internally,
   first meeting, possibly second meeting. We want Novo's
   institutional process to be observable (a meeting on calendar,
   an NDA in motion, a routing signal from corp dev to R&D) before
   we open the Lilly conversation.

2. **Short enough for the parallel to be visible to both.** If the
   Lilly conversation starts 90 days after Novo, Novo's process is
   already mid-LOI when Lilly enters; the parallel-bidder dynamic
   triggers at the wrong stage. The optimal moment is when both
   conversations are in the "active first-meeting" stage, which
   happens with a 30-day gap.

3. **Coordinated calendar around JP Morgan Healthcare Conference.**
   The conference week is the structural deadline. If JPM is the
   first week of January 2027, the Novo cold email goes out in
   late October 2026 and the Lilly cold email goes out in late
   November 2026. Both first meetings happen at JPM. The parallel
   is visible inside the conference.

By Week 12 (3 months from start), the parallel-bidder dynamic is
real and observable. Both acquirers are in the same conversation
stage. Neither has the deal locked. Both know the other is in the
room without us having to explicitly tell them. That's the
operational sweet spot.

## Section 4 — The "don't get killed by Noom" angle

The single largest existential threat to this strategy is not Novo
saying no. It's Noom Med signing an exclusive partnership with
either Novo or Lilly before COYL is in the room.

### Why Noom is the threat, not the bidder

Noom Med (the pharma-integrated layer of Noom) is a coaching-based,
weekly-cadence behavioral support program. It is architecturally
different from COYL (runtime behavioral interrupt vs weekly coaching
program). But from the acquirer's perspective at first read, both
are "behavioral support adjacent to GLP-1." The acquirer's diligence
team will compare them.

The threat is asymmetric:
- If Noom Med signs exclusive with Novo, Lilly has Noom Med
  alternatives (Calibrate, Sequence) — Lilly's path collapses
  partially, not entirely.
- If Noom Med signs exclusive with Lilly, Novo also has alternatives.
- If Noom Med signs exclusive with EITHER, COYL's parallel-bidder
  dynamic collapses entirely. The remaining acquirer has a
  reference comp ("we already saw Noom; here's the price") that
  caps COYL's strategic premium at the Noom benchmark.

### The Noom-watching intelligence priority

This is Intelligence Priority #1 for the next 90 days. Specifically:

1. **Monitor Noom + Noom Med public communications.** Investor
   relations announcements, press releases, earnings call language.
   Particular flags: "strategic pharma partnership," "GLP-1
   maintenance program partnership," "outcomes-based pharma deal."

2. **Monitor pharma + Noom relationship signals.** Look for: shared
   conference speaker slots between Noom Med leadership and Novo/
   Lilly Strategy leadership. Joint white papers. Common advisory
   board members.

3. **Talk to the network.** A16z (Noom investor adjacency), General
   Catalyst (multiple digital health relationships) — discreet
   queries through warm channels about whether Noom is in
   conversations with Novo or Lilly. Not direct asks; soft
   intelligence gathering.

4. **Read the trade press.** Endpoints News, STAT News, Fierce
   Pharma. The first signal of a Noom + pharma deal will leak
   through industry trade press 4-8 weeks before any formal
   announcement.

### What we do if Noom signs first

The contingency plan:
- (a) Pivot away from the standalone-pharma-acquirer thesis. The
  $4-6B band collapses to $1.5-2.5B.
- (b) Pursue the Microsoft Viva tech-platform path as primary (see
  Section 5 below + the engineering spec).
- (c) Accelerate the consumer + enterprise revenue build to the
  point where COYL is a standalone IPO candidate (Series B
  trajectory, $40M+ ARR, FDA path active).
- (d) Reopen the pharma conversation as a Series B investor (not
  acquirer) — Novo Holdings + Lilly Ventures could both still be
  investors in a Series B even if neither is the strategic
  acquirer.

The Noom risk is real and active. The defensive moves above are
ready but not yet triggered. Trigger is: any credible signal of a
Noom + Novo or Noom + Lilly partnership announcement within the
next 6 months.

## Section 5 — The Microsoft Viva moonshot (parallel tech-platform track)

Even with pharma as the primary path, Microsoft Viva stays alive as
a parallel track. The two paths don't kill each other — they price
on different value logic and operate on different timelines.

### Why both paths can run in parallel

The valuation logic is structurally different:

| Path | Value driver | Valuation logic | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pharma (Novo/Lilly) | Behavioral adjunct to drug | Strategic-bidder premium on cost-of-losing GLP-1 retention edge | 12-18 months to LOI |
| Tech platform (Microsoft Viva) | Enterprise productivity behavioral layer | Platform-acquisition multiple on enterprise PMPM TAM | 18-24 months to LOI |

The pharma acquirer values COYL as an obesity-cardiometabolic asset.
The tech-platform acquirer values COYL as an enterprise productivity
platform. Different categories, different value logic, no zero-sum
conflict in positioning.

The parallel can be operationally challenging — managing two
strategic conversations across two industries — but it's not
structurally impossible. The risk is dilution of focus, not category
conflict.

### How the Microsoft track is operationalized

See `../integrations/microsoft-viva-engineering-spec.md` for the
technical integration spec. Operationally:

1. **The Viva partner application is already in flight** (see
   `microsoft-viva-partner-application.md`).
2. **The BD script for Viva remains active** (see
   `bd-script-microsoft-viva.md`) but is de-prioritized to a slower
   cadence — one Viva touchpoint per 30 days, vs. the weekly
   touchpoint cadence of the pharma path.
3. **The engineering integration spec** (the file referenced above)
   is what Microsoft's Viva engineering team reads after the BD
   conversation. It exists so that when Microsoft is ready to move
   from BD to pilot, the technical conversation can move fast.
4. **The Microsoft conversation is a hedge.** If both pharma paths
   stall or Noom Med signs first, Microsoft Viva becomes the
   primary path within 60-90 days.

### The Microsoft Viva valuation band

- Probability of $4-8B outcome through Microsoft path: 8-15%
- Probability of $4-6B outcome through pharma path: 17-25%
- Probability of $1-2B outcome through any path: 30-40%
- Probability of no exit (continue to Series B + build): 30-50%

Pharma is primary because the probability-weighted value is highest
there. Microsoft is parallel because the cost of keeping it warm is
low (one BD touch per month) and the optionality is real.

## Section 6 — Conflict management (how to keep Novo + Lilly both in the room without lying)

The hardest operational problem in this strategy is the conflict
management between Novo and Lilly. Both will ask, directly or
indirectly, whether we're talking to the other. The wrong answer
kills the deal in either room.

### The principle: never specifically name, never specifically deny

The framing in every room is identical:

> "We're having parallel conversations with one other Top-2 obesity
> pharma. Not pushing urgency — flagging timing because the field
> is moving fast and I'd rather you have context now."

Three things are true about this framing:
- (a) It does not lie. We literally are having parallel
  conversations.
- (b) It does not specifically name. We never say "Lilly" in the
  Novo room, or "Novo" in the Lilly room. Naming converts to
  coercion + signals desperation.
- (c) It does not specifically deny. We never say "we are not
  talking to Lilly" in the Novo room — that's the lie that kills
  the deal when it's later proven false.

### The corner-case scenarios

**Scenario A — Novo's corp dev directly asks: "Are you talking to
Lilly?"**

Response: "We're having parallel conversations with one other Top-2
obesity pharma. I'm not going to specifically name them — that's
not respectful to them, and I wouldn't tell them specifically about
this conversation either. What I can tell you is that the
conversation is at a similar stage, the strategic frame is similar,
and the parallel pressure is real."

This response is honest, doesn't name, doesn't deny, and respects
the other party. Pharma corp dev appreciates discretion. They will
read this as "yes, it's Lilly" and they'll respect us more for
not saying so.

**Scenario B — Lilly's CEO asks David Ricks-style: "What's the
real number you'd take from Novo today?"**

Response: "I don't have a real number from Novo today. We're at the
first-meeting stage with them too. The reason I'm in this room is
because the conversation needs to be parallel. Lilly cannot be the
second mover here — that's the worst outcome for Lilly and that's
not why I'm here."

This response acknowledges the parallel without making up numbers.
Making up a Novo number is the fastest way to lose Lilly when Lilly
back-channels and learns the number is fictional.

**Scenario C — Either side asks for exclusivity at any stage before
the LOI.**

Response: "No exclusivity before LOI. The Path A LOI structure
includes a 12-month exclusivity period during which you cannot
acquire or invest in a direct competitor. The exclusivity travels
with the LOI, not with the early conversation. If you want
exclusivity earlier, the price is the LOI itself."

This response converts exclusivity asks into deal-acceleration asks.
If they want exclusivity, they should accelerate to LOI. If they
don't want to accelerate, they don't want exclusivity badly enough
to deserve it.

### When the conflict escalates: the M&A advisor's role

The Qatalyst advisor (when engaged) becomes the buffer between
direct conflict signals. Specifically:

1. Qatalyst can have parallel banker-to-corp-dev conversations
   with both Novo and Lilly that the founder cannot. Bankers
   running parallel processes is normal industry practice.
2. Qatalyst signals timing and pressure to both sides without us
   having to. "We have a window opening with the asset; you should
   move."
3. When the conflict escalates beyond what the founder can
   manage gracefully, Qatalyst takes over. The founder retreats to
   product + clinical conversations; the banker runs the deal
   tempo.

## Section 7 — Walk-away: the $4B floor

The walk-away floor is $4B. Below this, we walk and continue
building independently.

### Why $4B specifically

The math:
- Series A at $18-22M post-money at ~$100M pre-money: 10-14% dilution
- Series B at $40-60M post-money at ~$300-500M pre-money: 10-15%
  dilution
- Founder + early team retention: ~50-55% post-Series-B
- $4B exit, 50% founder/team retention: $2B distributed to founder
  + team

A $2-3B distribution at the founder + team level is the threshold
where the acquisition outcome exceeds the expected value of
continuing to build through to IPO. Below $4B, the expected value
of building independently exceeds the acquisition value.

### What "walk away" actually means

Walking away is not "say no and disappear." It's:

1. (a) Decline the LOI at the current price band
2. (b) Continue advancing both pharma conversations to the next
   milestone (RCT data, 500K users, 10K paying users)
3. (c) Open Series B raise in parallel (signals independence and
   creates new valuation reference)
4. (d) Reopen the acquisition conversation 6-12 months later at the
   higher valuation justified by the new milestones

The walk is a pause, not an end. The signal of the walk — "we'd
rather build than sell at this price" — often creates the
counteroffer that crosses the floor.

### Below the floor: build through to Series B + IPO trajectory

If both pharma paths stall AND Microsoft stalls AND no acquisition
crosses $4B:

- Continue executing Strategy v3
- Series B at Month 30-36 at $300-500M pre
- IPO trajectory at $40M+ ARR with FDA filing active
- Reopen strategic conversations as a Series B investor relationship
  (Novo Holdings, Lilly Ventures, Microsoft M12 — all are
  potential investors in a Series B even if none is the
  acquirer)

The build-through path is the bear case but it's not a failure
case. The bear case is a $500M-$1.5B outcome at IPO + secondary,
which is still meaningful for the founder + team.

## Section 8 — Banker selection: Qatalyst vs Allen & Co

The recommendation is Qatalyst Partners. The reasoning:

### Why Qatalyst

1. **Digital health expertise.** Quattrone's firm has been in the
   room for multiple digital-health-to-pharma deals (Livongo,
   Teladoc, Welltok). The diligence questions are the same; the
   precedents are documented.

2. **Strategic-bidder process expertise.** Qatalyst's reputation is
   built on running auction processes that maximize the strategic-
   bidder premium. They are the firm that other firms call when
   they need to maximize a price.

3. **Speed.** Qatalyst runs faster cycles than the relationship-
   driven boutiques. We need speed — the 12-18 month window from
   Section 2 doesn't allow for 12-month banker engagements.

4. **The fee structure.** Qatalyst's fee is 1-2% of deal value with
   a $1-2M minimum retainer. At a $4B deal, that's $40-80M to the
   banker — but the alternative (running the auction ourselves) is
   leaving $400M+ on the table. The banker pays for themselves
   easily at this scale.

### Why not Allen & Co

Allen & Co is relationship-first. The Sun Valley conference network,
the Herb Allen-style "we know everyone" approach. Two reasons it's
the wrong fit:

1. (a) The relationships are skewed toward old-media and consumer.
   Allen & Co's pharma relationships are real but secondary; their
   first-call relationships are at Disney, Comcast, Sony.
2. (b) The pace is slower. Allen-style deals develop over 18-24
   months of relationship-building. We don't have that window.

If Qatalyst signals a conflict (they're advising Novo or Lilly on
something else and won't take our side), the second option is
Centerview. Not Allen.

### When to engage the banker

Engage Qatalyst after the first Novo cold email but before the
first Novo call. Specifically:

- Week 0: Send Novo cold email (Section 3 sequence)
- Week 0-1: Engage Qatalyst on retainer ($150-250K initial retainer,
  3-month engagement scope)
- Week 1-2: Qatalyst opens parallel channel into Novo
- Week 2-4: First Novo call (we run; Qatalyst observes)
- Week 4+: Qatalyst transitions to active deal-running role

The 3-month engagement scope is intentional. We don't commit to
12 months upfront — Qatalyst earns the extended engagement by
showing the parallel-bidder dynamic is real and working.

## Section 9 — Timing benchmarks (pharma corp dev cadence)

Pharma corp dev moves at a quarterly pace by default. Specifically:

| Stage | Timing (typical) | Timing (aggressive) |
|---|---|---|
| First cold email → first meeting | 4-6 weeks | 2-3 weeks |
| First meeting → second meeting | 4 weeks | 1-2 weeks |
| Second meeting → NDA signed | 4-6 weeks | 2 weeks |
| NDA → Tier 2 diligence complete | 6-8 weeks | 3-4 weeks |
| Tier 2 diligence → LOI conversation | 4 weeks | 1-2 weeks |
| LOI conversation → signed LOI | 4-8 weeks | 2-4 weeks |
| Signed LOI → close | 90-180 days | 60-90 days |

The total: 60-90 days from first conversation to LOI in the
aggressive case. 120-180 days in the typical case. The COYL
strategy assumes the aggressive case is achievable because:
- (a) Lilly's documented track record (Loxo, Dermira)
- (b) Qatalyst's process management
- (c) The competitive parallel-bidder pressure shortens cycles on
  both sides

The pharma acquisition close (LOI → cash in the bank) typically
takes 4-6 months on top of the LOI signing. So a "deal closes in 12
months from first conversation" is the realistic aggressive case.
