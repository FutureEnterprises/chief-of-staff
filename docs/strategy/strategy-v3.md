# Founder Strategy Brief v3

**COYL — The Path from $0 → $100M ARR → $1.5–4B Valuation**
*Stop the script before it runs your life.*

Third revision. v1 was a pitch. v2 fixed the structural arguments. v3
calibrates the numbers, adds the team / cost / risk lines that survive
diligence, and reframes the category as a decision-interrupt layer

The 4B outcome is real. The timeline is one year longer than aggressive

---

## Section 1 — The reframe

COYL is not a behavioral-health app. Behavioral-health apps compete
with Noom, Calm, BetterHelp, Headspace — a crowded, low-trust category
with a $1–2B ceiling and brutal churn. That's the wrong category.

**COYL is a decision-interrupt layer.** A real-time substrate that
fires in the 3-second window between trigger and action. Category-
agnostic in architecture, with four monetization paths:

1. **Consumer D2C** — subscription for individuals
2. **Enterprise / EAP displacement** — PEPM for employers
3. **Clinical / Payer** — FDA-cleared digital therapeutic with payer
   reimbursement (multiplier, not engine — see §5)
4. **Behavioral interrupt API** — platform layer licensed to other
   apps in adjacent categories (gambling, BNPL, social media, sobriety)

The reframe matters because every other piece of the strategy compounds
off it. Enterprise pricing, payer reimbursement multiples, API
partnerships, and the FDA pathway all become legible *only* under the
decision-interrupt framing. As a "behavior change app," none of those
channels are accessible.

## Section 2 — The defensible TAM

Not 5B people. The intersection of three filters:

1. Measurable autopilot-driven cost (weight, money, relationships,
   productivity loss)
2. Smartphone access + ability/willingness to pay
3. Already spending money on solutions that work *after* the behavior,
   not before

**English-speaking market: ~180–250M addressable users today.**
Global (10-year horizon, emerging markets): ~600–800M.

This is the population already paying for:
- Noom, Calibrate, Found ($1B+ category)
- EAP services ($5.1B US, 3–6% utilization)
- Habit tracking + productivity apps ($2B+ category)
- Therapy + behavioral health ($35B US)
- Sobriety + addiction recovery apps (small but growing)

**The framing isn't "acquiring a new behavior." It's "displacing $12B+
already being spent on solutions that intervene after the moment."**

That's a defensible, specific TAM that survives diligence. Don't lead
with the bigger number.

## Section 3 — The four-layer architecture

### Layer 1 — Consumer D2C (Year 1–2 cash engine)

What it is: a single horizontal product that works for any autopilot
script the user defines. Three primary wedges today:

- Weight loss / GLP-1 maintenance
- Procrastination / focus
- Destructive loops (cravings, doom-scroll, impulse spending)

**Pricing — two scenarios:**

| Lane | **Scenario B (LIVE)** | Scenario A (upmarket fallback) |
|---|---|---|
| Free | Audit + archetype + 1 behavior loop | 7-day trial |
| Core | **$9.99/mo** ($99/yr) | $19/mo ($179/yr) |
| GLP-1 / high-intent | **$19.99/mo — GLP-1 Companion** | $29/mo Plus + $49/mo Pro |

Scenario B is what's live on coyl.ai today. It is acquisition-pricing,
not LTV-maximizing. The $9.99 Core is calibrated to the virality
coefficient of the free audit funnel; the $19.99 GLP-1 tier is where
the paid ARPU concentrates because willingness-to-pay tracks the
medical / aesthetic stakes of weight maintenance.

Scenario A is the trade-up path if consumer pricing power proves
higher than projected after Month 3 of paid-user data. Existing users
grandfathered; new cohorts re-priced.

**Y1 targets:**
- Scenario B: ~250K free signups + 10K paid (mix-shifted to $19.99 tier
  at ~35%) ≈ $1.4M ARR run-rate by month 12, ~$3.5M GAAP Y1 with ramp
- Scenario A: 100K free signups + 4K paid = $1M MRR ($12M ARR run-rate
  by month 12, ~$5M GAAP Y1 with ramp)

The consumer line funds operations and builds the dataset that
underwrites Layers 2–4. It's the cash engine, not the valuation engine.
**In Scenario B the dataset compounds 2.5× faster because the free
audit funnel sweeps more users — this is the strategic argument for
launching with the lower price.**

### Layer 2 — Enterprise / EAP displacement (Year 1–3)

What it is: COYL as a real-time replacement for EAP. Current EAP at
~$2 PEPM has 3–6% utilization because it's passive (call a number,
wait for a session). COYL is the only EAP-adjacent product that fires
at the moment of behavior.

**Pricing trajectory:**
- Y1 pilots: $3–5 PEPM (earning the price)
- Y2 with early outcome data: $5–8 PEPM
- Y3 with clinical validation: $8–15 PEPM (target band)

**The trojan horse:** displace the EAP entirely, don't sit alongside.
Every EAP contract renewal (2-year cycle) is a displacement window.

Y3 enterprise target: 50–80 logos × $25–40K ACV = $1.5–3M GAAP per
quarter, ramping to ~$28M Y3 ARR. Requires 3–4 BD reps + ~$1.5–2M Y2
GTM investment. **The cost side is real and must be in the model.**

### Layer 3 — Clinical / Payer (Year 3–4 multiplier, NEVER lifeline)

**The Pear Therapeutics lesson is mandatory reading for everyone on
this team.** Pear had FDA-cleared digital therapeutics, real
reimbursement codes, real clinical data — and went bankrupt in 2023
because payer adoption was slower than burn rate.

The lesson is not "avoid the clinical path." It's: **the clinical /
payer channel must be funded by a deliberately oversized Series A and
must never be the primary cash engine.** It's a multiplier on a
business that already works without it.

**Honest timeline:**

| Milestone | Timing |
|---|---|
| IRB approval + RCT enrollment starts | Month 3–4 |
| 12-week active intervention complete | Month 7–8 |
| Follow-up + data analysis | Month 9–12 |
| Paper submitted / preprint live | Month 13–15 |
| FDA De Novo filing submitted | Month 16–18 |
| FDA clearance | Month 28–36 |
| First payer reimbursement negotiation | Month 30–42 |
| First payer revenue | Late Year 3 to Year 4 |

Y4 clinical target: $2–5M from first 1–3 payer contracts. Meaningful
contribution comes in Year 5+.

### Layer 4 — Behavioral interrupt API (Year 2–4)

**Critical correction from v1: Month 3 is the LOI, not the shipped
API.** The platform thesis becomes investor-credible at Month 3
through a signed LOI with a sobriety app (Reframe or Monument are
right targets — small enough to move fast, desperate enough to try).

**API timeline:**

- Month 3: First LOI signed (sobriety app)
- Month 6–9: Build the category-agnostic substrate (2–3 senior
  engineers, 6 months). The current COYL interrupt engine is
  COYL-shaped; making it accept category parameters is real
  engineering work.
- Month 10–12: Private API beta with the LOI partner
- Month 18–24: GA launch to 3–5 partners
- Year 3: $4–8M ARR from API
- Year 4: $12–18M ARR

Target partners by category:
- **Sobriety:** Reframe, Monument, Sober Grid
- **Gambling:** DraftKings, FanDuel (regulatory pressure makes
  responsible-gambling tools a procurement priority, not a feature)
- **BNPL / fintech:** Klarna, Affirm, Cash App (impulse spending is
  their biggest churn driver and biggest regulatory exposure)
- **Social media:** Instagram, TikTok (doom-scroll interrupt will be
  regulatorily mandated eventually)
- **Dating:** Reactive messaging is a use case millions would pay to
  interrupt

**Reading these tables honestly:**

- **Scenario B lands at ~75% of Scenario A's Y5 ARR.** Lower per-user
  ARPU, ~2.5× the paid users; enterprise / API / clinical lines do
  not change because the price-point shift only re-prices the consumer

  consumer mix is heavier in the cap table; the FDA / payer multiple
  on the other lines stays the same.
- **The launch decision is strategic, not financial.** Scenario B
  trades ~25% of Y5 ARR for ~2.5× the dataset that underwrites the API
  moat (Layer 4) and the strategic-acquisition optionality (the $4B
  story is behavior graph + category control, not ARPU).
- **$4B is a Year 5 number, not Year 4.** Pushing it to Year 5 reads
  as discipline, not weakness. Scenario A Y4 = $1.7B; Scenario B Y4 =

- **The Y5 valuation expands** because by then FDA clearance is real,
  payer revenue is meaningful, and the multiple legitimately
  compresses to a healthcare-grade 17–22×.
- **Consumer + enterprise carry the first three years.** Don't
  underwrite payer revenue in any pre-Series-B model.
- **Y2 burn is similar in both scenarios.** Scenario B has higher
  paid-user count (2.5×) but lower per-user support cost (lower price,
  lower expectation surface); Scenario A has higher CAC and higher
  support cost per user. Burn lands within ~10% across scenarios.

## Section 5 — The Pear Therapeutics lesson, internalized

Pear had:
- FDA clearance for reSET (substance use disorder, 2017)
- FDA clearance for reSET-O (opioid use disorder, 2018)
- Somryst clearance (insomnia, 2020)
- Reimbursement codes
- Published RCTs
- $200M+ in funding

Pear went bankrupt in 2023. The cause: payer adoption was slower than
projected, the company's burn rate assumed payer revenue ramping in
years 3–4, and when payer ramp slipped, the cash ran out before the
revenue arrived.

**The lesson is not "avoid FDA."** The lesson is:
1. Never let clinical/payer revenue be the primary cash engine
2. Underwrite the Series A to survive the worst payer-adoption case
3. Keep consumer + enterprise as the survival floor through the dead
   zone (Months 24–36)

This shapes the capital plan in §7.

## Section 6 — The platform argument (network effects, earned)

The "platform" word has to earn itself. Most "platform" pitches are
just multiple products in one company. Real platforms have network
effects — more users make the product better for every user.

**COYL's network effect:**

Every user's slip event (timestamp + context + outcome) sharpens the
timing model for every other user in the same archetype × danger-
window × wedge. After 100K users, the model knows:

- 73% of Night Fridge Saboteurs slip between 9:47–10:18 PM on weekdays
  with stress-text-received-within-prior-2-hours
- Post-GLP-1 users in week 14 have a slip-cluster between days 17–22
- Procrastination archetypes have a tab-switch frequency that predicts
  next-hour collapse with 81% accuracy

A new entrant with 5K users cannot replicate this model. The data is
the moat. The FDA path protects the moat. The API monetizes it.

**This is what justifies the 18–22× multiple in Year 5.** Pure SaaS
doesn't get those multiples. Platform with FDA-cleared moat does.

## Section 8 — What kills this plan

*Every $4B plan needs this slide. Naming risks pre-empts investor
objections.*

**1. Clinical study null result (or worse, negative).**
- Probability: 15–25%
- Mitigation: pre-register the SAP at OSF, design the study for
  effect-size estimation (not powered for null). Even a "no effect"
  finding has scientific value and the consumer + enterprise lines
  survive without payer.

**2. App Store / Play Store rejection on behavioral-health-adjacent
grounds.**
- Probability: 10–15% (one rejection cycle, recoverable)
- Mitigation: "behavioral support, not medical treatment"
  framing throughout. App Store guideline 5.1.1(v) compliance shipped
  (account deletion). Preflight script catches config issues.

**3. Stripe / payment regulatory issue with the financial-stakes
mechanic.**
- Probability: 5–10%
- Mitigation: stakes is Premium-tier (small user base initially),
  funds go to a registered 501(c)(3) charity (GiveDirectly) via
  Stripe Connect Transfer. No "anti-charity" until regulatory
  review clears it.

**4. Consumer retention misses 45%.**
- Probability: 30–40% (this is the real silent bet)
- Mitigation: measurement gate at Month 3 of paid-user data.
  If retention < 35%, reduce CAC or pause paid acquisition.
  Series A sized to survive a 2× ramp-down on consumer.

**5. Pear scenario — payer dead zone outlasts cash.**
- Probability: 25–35%
- Mitigation: Series A explicitly sized for 24–30 month runway,
  consumer + enterprise floor maintained at $35M+ Y3 to absorb
  the entire burn even if payer revenue is $0.

**6. Solo-founder ceiling reached at $5M ARR.**
- Probability: ~50% if no early senior hire
- Mitigation: Seed funds the first senior engineer in Month 0.
  Founder bottleneck addressed before Y1 ends.
